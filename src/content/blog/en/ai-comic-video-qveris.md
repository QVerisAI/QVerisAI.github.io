# Why Did AI Comic Dramas Suddenly Take Over My Short Videos? I Asked QVeris to Check

# I Just Watched One, and the Platform Thinks I’ll Watch All Night

Recently, while scrolling through short videos, I encountered content that’s increasingly hard to categorize.

It looks like animation, but the pacing and dialogue follow short drama rhythms; characters aren’t always natural, yet emotions run high; episodes might last only minutes, with titles always featuring rebirth, revenge, cultivation, systems, aristocratic families, or “Everyone looks down on me.”

After clicking one, the recommendation page quickly showed a second, then a third. Before I could recognize the live actors, I was already following an AI male lead who might not even exist.

This content is commonly called AI comic dramas. Recent public reports have also frequently discussed its growth on short drama charts, as well as the concentrated entry of platforms, copyright holders, and content companies.

But as an ordinary user, what truly intrigues me isn’t “How big is the AI comic drama market?” but several more direct questions:

> What are people actually watching?
> 
> Which genres showed higher engagement in this sample?
> 
> Can public engagement data reveal signals of users actively following, saving, or discussing?
> 
> Who is behind these contents?

So I turned to QVeris with this question.

# Step 1: Transform “AI Comic Drama” from a Term into Real Content

Instead of having an Agent write an industry analysis first, I asked QVeris to find actual AI comic drama content available on public platforms.

In this test, QVeris returned content titles, author accounts, publication times, topic tags, and public engagement metrics like likes, saves, comments, and shares.

This step was crucial.

Without real content samples, so-called “trend analysis” could easily become a few correct but vague statements: AI lowers production costs, short dramas suit fragmented consumption, users prefer strong emotions… These judgments might not be wrong, but they still don’t answer what people are actually watching.

QVeris retrieved 20 results in this test. After relevance filtering, a cross-platform sample pool was established with 8 Xiaohongshu (RED) contents and 8 Douyin (TikTok) contents.

# Step 2: Hit Genres Might Be More “Honest” Than I Imagined

After watching many, I realized AI comic dramas don’t hide their emotional formulas.

Characters can start miserable, identity reversals must happen fast, and conflicts should occur within the first thirty seconds. If the protagonist hasn’t been kicked out of the house, broken off an engagement, awakened a system, or discovered a hidden identity by the first episode, I’d even question if it’s the wrong show.

Of course, this is just a feeling from scrolling. To truly assess genre trends, data is needed.

QVeris allows Agents to cluster titles, descriptions, and topic tags in the sample, then tally:

This yields more than just “AI comic dramas are popular”—it gives specific conclusions: which genres are most common, which drive higher engagement, and which content merely has high volume but average user response.

# Step 3: Likes Are an Attitude, Comments Are the Real Live Feed

Whether content can be produced is one thing; whether users will follow is another.

The comment section of AI comic dramas might be more interesting than the videos themselves. Some care about plot, others mock characters’ fingers, some can’t stand lip-syncing, while others don’t care about visual authenticity and just want more episodes.

So I continued having QVeris compile public comments and engagement signals, separately observing what users praise, criticize, and whether clear demands like urging for more episodes, requesting tutorials, or seeking resources appear.

This requires extreme restraint. Agents shouldn’t turn dozens of comments into “all users unanimously agree,” but should clearly label sample scope and platform sources.

- Whether copyright partnerships, IP adaptations, or platform collaborations exist
